Mobile, AL

Say the word "automobile" aloud. Listen carefully to those last two syllables. Chop off the first two and you're left with the correct way to pronounce the name of Alabama's only port city. This lesson in southern elocution was graciously given to us by Bill and Wendy X who are hosting us at their home, the lovely Kate Shepard House located in--say it with us--Mobile, Alabama.

We chose this as a stop on our way to New Orleans. And we chose it in an attempt to avoid the madness that is Mardi Gras in New Orleans. Far be it from me to rain on anyone's parade, but Mardi Gras always looked like exactly the kind of instiutionalized baccanal best avoided. Travelling south at this time of year with plans to visit New Orleans, meant figuring out where else to spend Fat Tuesday. A little research, and we found Mobile claimed to have been the birthplace of the celebration. Good enough for us.

We were lucky to find The Kate Shepard House. After too many nights in fancy schmancy hotels it was a relief to be in someone's home. And Bill and Wendy not only allowed Rufus, they were excited to have him as a guest. They're the proud owners of two year-old Koa, a sweet Chow who loves having another dog in the house. I gave Ru the billing he deserves--as a good, well behaved pooch who has manners that make him inn and hotel worthy. With rare exceptions, Rufus is excited to be in the presence of other dogs. So it came of something of a shock when we entered the inn, Koa and Rufus met, and Rufus tuned into a growly, snappish pill.

The innkeepers couldn't have been nicer about it, suggesting Ru was tired from his travels and would feel more hospitibily inclined toward Koa the next day. I wondered if he was worried about being pounced on by a pup, due to his recent shoulder injury. But whatever it was, it was behavior we hadn't previously seen and we were mortified. Here were these nice, nice folks offering up their nice, nice dog as a playmate. They had every right to think we were a delusional people who thought their nasty little dog was well trained.

After a good night's sleep, we were all in better stead to meet up again. Rufus, Koa, Bill and Eric went for a stroll around the neighborhood, and Rufus came to accept that Koa might be OK company after all. At breakfast (eggs and garlic grits--yummy!) we spent time chatting with the inn's only other guest: Lowell, an older gentleman farmer from Ohio who loved the blues and had been going to Mardi Gras celebrations in New Orleans for decades. Last year, he found the grand mal celebration lacking and decided to come to Mobile in search of missing merriment. According to Lowell, who was dripping in easily 10 pounds worth of plastic bling thrown from parade floats, Mobile's celebration was smaller, but more managable.

With Ru and Koa now friends, and Bill and Wendy generously offering to watch him, we set of for a day of prowling the city and experiencing Fat Tuesday, Mobile-style. The weather was Seattlesque: a misty rain followed us throughout the day. We gambolled from neighborhood to neighborhood, ogling the lovely old homes and cozying up to barricades when we'd run into a parade, which was frequently. In fact, the parades are constant. And, just like pictures or film you might have seen, they're filled with themed floats, Mardi Gras Kings and Queens and their courts flinging plastic beads, candy, Moon Pies, stuffed animals and all manner of loot out into the crowds. We'd read in the papers of one float that was planning on throwing packages of Ramen--economical and nutritious! Motorcycle cops sped by, beads swinging from their bikes making sure all appropriate body parts were covered. (The only bare breasts we saw were replicas on a string of Mardi Gras beads.)

We'd been warned by our hosts that some folks too this thing much to seriously and would just about grab stuff out of your hands as you caught it, but this wasn't our experience. When we got hungry or cold, we'd peel off into a neighborhood, looking for coffee or grub. The air was thick with smoke from countless barbecues; people were grilling up and down neighborhood streets. The city was absolutely shut down, except for bars and restaurants, where the party was in full swing. Funky stands sold grilled homemade food and all manner of food on a stick, the most popular being the time-tested corn dog and the nouvelle interloper: the fried twinkie. (We passed on both, going for oyster po' boys at a great old in a funky nabe.)

Travelling by horse and wagon. Need a wife, Call Cowboy Cupid.
