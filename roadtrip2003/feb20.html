Feb 20th-24th--Sarasota, FL

Rolling through Florida, we're disappointed that it's the most challenging of all the states we've visited for finding dog-friendly accommodations. Generally, we tried to balance advance research and reservations with some flex. As it's prime-time for Florida tourism, we're bitten in the butt by this strategy. We'd hoped to get out to the Keys, but have little luck in uncovering anywhere that isn't fully booked that will take Rufus. We have a near miss with a lovely sounding little guest house that perches on a quiet beach and loans kayaks. But it's not to be. Failing to turn up anything more interesting, we're trapped in the velvet prison that is The Ritz. And even then, only some Ritzes will take him. So, onward and westward. Next stop--Sarasota.

We tour quickly through South Beach, wowed by the deco architecture and marking it as a place worthy of visiting again. And then we cut across the swamplands toward our next destination. If I were to return to Florida, this is a place I'd *definitely* spend more time. Eric is spotting alligators from our car as we drive through the aptly named "Alligator Alley," heading for the gulf-side of Florida. I see a flamingo, and unlike those I've met previously, it can fly!

We'd been looking forward to stopping for a few days, to walking some beaches and curling up with books and newspapers. This was the part of the trip where we planned on recharging--no visiting, no schedules. But as with so many things in life, what's going to happen isn't neccesarily what you've planned.

We check into the Sarasota Ritz and it's instantly winning points for running well. It's a big, new shiny hotel with too much marble and too many chandeliers. But the staff get the service thing and do it well, giving Rufus a warm welcome, so that's a happy thing. The king has had supper and has developed the rituals of an old man dog. After he eats dinner he wants to go to bed. And that means getting lifted up onto the bed, which we obligingly do when he woofs at it. An attentive maid noticed something missing from the bathroom when she was turning down the room, and returns with it. When she knocks on the door, Rufus feels obliged to bark, bounce, and warn us of a potential intruder. She knocks, he instantly flings himself off the bed and lands badly. In a split second we become people with a bud vase and a dog with an injured front leg.

We feel around carefully on his shoulder, leg, and back and he doesn't react with any obvious pain. We doubt there are any fractures. But he's limping badly--it's probably a sprain. We decide to give it the night. By morning, he's still a gimp boy. So, we find a dog-loving concierge and make an appointment that afternoon at her veterinarian. Poor Ru. Most dogs wind up injured in more heroic ways--chasing another pooch, or simply running full-out. Our little guy has to suffer the indignity of an injury received in the line of duty, living a life of too much privilege, jumping off a bed at the Ritz.

We pull up to the vet's, somewhere in strip mall land in Sarasota. The technician working the front desk makes heavy weather of the fact that even though Ru's rabies vaccine is current in 49 of the 50 United States, in Florida, he's due for one. He needs records of the almost two year-old vaccine faxed from our Seattle vet, which we arrange for on our cell. And then he drops the whole thing as an issue. Whatever. Oprah is blaring from a TV set in the corner. No one is watching and Ru is flinching at the yelps coming from the back of the hospital, tugging at his leash to go out the door, knowing that's the way to GET OUT OF HERE.  Even though we have an appointment, we wait more than an hour for Ru to see the doc, who is a very young and very kind Doogie Houser sort of vet. While we wait, an elderly woman with a raven-black architectural beehive of a 'do chats me up. She loved her dog so, but he died, she says. Just like her two husbands. So she doesn't want another--of either. Breaks your heart. She's just there with her friend who brought a cat in to be treated.

"How long have you lived in Florida?" I ask.

"Moved here in the 60's," she said. "Left my job and apartment in Manhattan and just went." When she smiles, you can see that as a young woman she was a stunner.

"Didn't have a job so I slept in the park," she continued, matter-of-factly.

Clearly, this is just the headlines.

"I went on lots of interviews, but nothing. Then, one day I see a job in the paper that pays $5 an hour. Who do you have to kill to get that one, I wondered?" she continued.

Turns out, the gig was developing leads for cemetery plot salesmen. She'd go door-to-door, looking for prospects.

"I'm really a shy person," she explained, "So I could only do it a couple of hours a day."

There wasn't a lot about her that communicated shyness, but two hours daily sounded like a fair limit for ringing doorbells to ask people if they'd thought about investing in a very, very small plot of real estate.

"At one house, I found a young guy. Handsome, too." she said. "So I left him my phone number. I didn't think he wanted a plot but I hoped he'd call."

He did. Asked her to dinner, giving her the choice of his house, her house, or a restaurant. She accepted, deciding she wanted to see if he could cook. THEN she let him take her to a restaurant. Then they married.

"I like to say I rang his bell from the moment we met," she smiled.

Ru is called into Doogie's inner sanctum. He's kind, gentle, Corgi mad and deeply apologetic for keeping us waiting for so long. After much careful feeling and prodding, he's pretty sure nothing is broken. He notes arthritis in Ru's legs and back and suggests a drug that will help that, and also help him heal up from this injury, which he thinks is a sprain. He suggests a steroid shot. I'm not so sure about that. And he suggests x-rays if we want to be sure nothing is broken. We say, yes--go ahead and x-ray. And off Ru limps to the dreaded back of the house. We wait quite a while and then Doogie comes out to ask if he can sedate the dog.

"Sedate like knock out or sedate like relax him a bit?" I asked.

"Relax him a bit," he replied.

"OK," we allowed and off Doogie goes.

Feeling uneasy, I slip out to the parking lot to call my vet in Seattle. She's great and drops everything to take my call. The Demax, the stuff for the arthritis makes sense, she says. The steroid shot doesn't--in fact, it would reduce the effectiveness of the Demax.

I go back inside and Doogie returns. I don't want to say I've called my vet in Seattle for a second opinion. So I say that I've had sports injuries myself--true--and that my doc warned me against steroidal shots for treatment--also true. It followed that I'd prefer he didn't give the dog the steroid shot.

No problem, he said. The problem is, Ru is giving them such a time that even mild sedation isn't going to do it for the x-ray. And he doesn't want to knock him all the way out. As he's pretty sure there isn't a break. Perhaps we'd want to skip the x-ray?

Makes sense to us.

Ru, who entered the back of the dog hospital limping like Grandpa McCoy now walks out of the torture zone perfectly normally. Whatever struggling he did with them back there has acted like some sort of involuntary chiropractic session and he seems magically better. We slip him Demax, just the same, and the limp only shows up again days later, when he romps with excitement over a particularly loving welcome he's given at hotel in Arizona. But we're getting ahead of ourselves.

By now, plot woman and her cat-loving friend have departed. Oprah has turned to local news. And Doogie doesn't want to charge us the full rate as we've been there for three hours. We insist on paying the entire balance. What a sweet man, but we want him to be able to afford to continue practicing.

Ru rests and continues to be well. The following day, we attempt beach and manage a nice stroll, but sun turns to rain, driving us off. That night, we explore Sarasota's downtown, finding a great, simple, funky Cuban restaurant (El Habanero on Burns Court) and a gallery district with a fine art house movie theater.

Stopping for couple of days has the contradictory effect of making us want to move on. And so, we do.
