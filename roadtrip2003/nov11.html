Nov. 10th & 11th, 2003 -- Kansas City, MO

What were we thinking--tomorrow St. Louis? We were so pooped we wound up staying three nights in Kansas City. We pulled in late, feeling punchy from a long day's drive. Much to our delight, our fancy, 60's-era hotel was perched on the edge of The Plaza, a district of historic, Moorish-influenced buildings that now house lots 'o shops. All was dramatically lit. Inexplicably, at the far end of the neighborhood, a bat-beam-esque shaft of light pointed skyward. I was smitten--this kind of architecture was generally reserved for old movie palaces. Here, they've lined the streets with it.

The next day, tromping around in a misty rain that would do Seattle proud, I couldn't have been more content to be in the state with the slogan that sounds like a taunt to a flasher. There were whimsical cupolas, intricately tiled facades, even a functioning carillon, dripping in filigree. The old buildings are beautifully restored; the more recent ones are often hard to distinguish from the originals. Who cares that they now house the likes of Williams-Sonoma, Barnes & Noble and The Sharper Image? It beats where these stores usually live. Even though it's a stone's throw from the geographical center of the lower 48, this city is distinctly not average.

Back at our hotel, we were assigned to "the dog floor," which is exactly what it sounds like: the floor where they stick you if you choose to take The Fairmont up on their dog-friendly policy. While comfortable, the room hadn't been updated in some time. That meant slightly tired furniture and indigo taffeta drapes that Scarlett O'Hara could have used in her ripping-down-the-curtains-to-make-a-gown scene. Also true to Scarlett's time: there was no internet access. (Though maddeningly, the rest of the hotel was wired.) The business center gouged the internet disenfranchised. In a bold move for freedom of information, we hightailed it to a nearby bakery-cafe with free Wi-Fi and happily spent a morning adding a couple of entries to the site and catching up on e-mail.

With that feather-light "homework done" feeling, we made our first quick touristy stop of the day: Hallmark. The card company is a huge employer in Kansas City and has been for almost a century. Say what you will about terrifying kitsch, saccharine sentiments and cheesy images--building a business on the Cyrano-like notion that most people have a hard time expressing their feelings is a brilliant move. And anything that gives artists and writers steady, paying work can't be all bad.

Company founder Joyce C. Hall had a vision for a downtown office, shopping, residential and entertainment complex with Hallmark at its center. The result: Crown Plaza. Much of it is sterile; some of it, almost mid-century hip. (And the 80's get their due: there's an I.M Pei tower.) It brought to mind Disney's vision for planned, contained communities. What was it about turn-of-the-century Missouri that made its most successful sons long for their own, highly-controlled little universes? (Walt grew up in Kansas City. His first animation studio, the birthplace of Mickey, Laugh-O-Gram, was born, and quickly died there.)

The Hallmark display tries to do for cards what The Smithsonian's National Museum of American History does for American pop culture. Photos, timelines and artifacts surround cards from the past century. There were toys. There were letters to famous contributors. (One from J.C. Hall to Norman Rockwell dubbed his Christmas card art a "lollapalooza.") There was even a strange bit of my own past: when I was 12 my mom bought me one of these.

It all works pretty well--these guys do get visual presentation. There are strange facts lurking here and there: who knew Winston Churchill painted? Hall found this out, befriended the old fellow, and eventually released six cards sporting Churchill's art. In fact, a Churchill seascape hangs at Hallmark. (It's just as well that he didn't quit his day job.) Though perhaps this is why this statue sat just down the street from our hotel. (Rufus loves a statesman.)

There were demonstrations of the printing process, explanations of die cutting, and a hall of glass booths, each airing a tear-jerker of a Hallmark Ad. In one, Mom finds card in son's backpack from son's teacher. Little boy to mom: "I didn't do anything special. He was too sick to go out to play, so I just stayed inside and played with him." Mom to little boy: "I don't know. I think that was special..." We moved quickly on for a short film on a retreat Hallmark sends its artists and writers to goose their creative juices, somewhere in Missouri farm country. Frankly, it looked fun. One exercise, that I've considered but haven't been able to fully answer: you're going on a journey, one from which you can't return and you won't see familiar surroundings again. What seven images would you bring with you? Try that one on. (Responses from you all are cheerfully accepted.)

By now, we were getting hungry. And after considerable research on competing barbecue joints (Bill Clinton is a Gates fan; Jimmy Carter and Calvin Trillin go for Arthur Bryant's) we took the tip from Jimmy and Calvin and were rewarded with a perfectly funky place serving all manner of barbecue. We opted for barbecued beef sandwiches that, as it turned out, could each easily have fed two or three people. For those keeping score, the original sauce was vinegary and not terribly sweet; the rich and spicy, more traditionally sweet and mildly hot. I managed to dispatch half of my sandwich; Eric downed his and the rest of mine and could still stand for tours of The Negro Leagues Baseball Museum and The American Jazz Museum. Both were swell, with baseball edging out jazz by a nose.

The museums share a new building at 18th and Vine, formerly the heart of Kansas City's African-American neighborhood. Across the street is the Gem, which, as you can see, still is one. Built in 1912, it's one of the country's few restored Black theaters. Inside the museums there are tons of artifacts, and a short, bittersweet film about the neighborhood during the years of the Negro Leagues and the early days of jazz. The devotion to the teams was massive. Churches would schedule services an hour earlier on Sundays with afternoon games. People dressed up in their best clothes to attend. No surprise: the history is sad and fascinating and full of stories you'd expect, but still, are heartbreaking to read. Other bits are stunners. This is a news report from an undated, uncredited paper:

    "Klan and Colored Team to Mix on the Diamond Today"

    "Strangle holds, razors, horsewhips and other violent implements of argument will be banned at the baseball game
    at Island Park this afternoon when the baseball club of the Wichita Klan Number Six goes up against the Wichita
    Monravians, Wichita's crack colored team..."

    "...The umpires have been instructed to rule any player out of the game who tries to bat with a cross..."

Interviews with players line the walls. Travel was particularly difficult. This, from Bill Yancey, of The New York Black Yankees:

    "I'll never forget the first time we went to West Virginia...there was no hotel where we could stay. It took
    maybe a couple of hours to find lodging for eight or nine fellows. One stay here, two stay here, like that..."

    "There was no place between Chicago and St. Louis where we could stop and eat--unless we stopped in a place
    where they had a colored settlement. From St. Louis to Kansas City, same thing. So, many times we would ride
    all night and not have anything to eat, because they wouldn't feed you."

Again--an uncredited newspaper, dated 1907, gave this headline and kicker to a story about the Kansas City Blue Belles:

    "Ball Team of Negro Girls
    Stranger Sees Practice of Aggregation of Colored Female Athletes"

And finally, there are Satchel Paige's Rules for the Good Life. My favorite is rule number 1:

    1. Avoid fried foods cause they angers up the blood.

Tough to argue with that.

Tomorrow on the road St. Louis. (This time, for sure.) (And here are the rest of Satchel's rules.)


Hours driven: less than 1, thank God
Number of museums toured: 3
Number of movie palaces spotted: 2
Number of iron statesmen Rufus sat on: 1
Snoozed at: The Fairmont Kansas City
