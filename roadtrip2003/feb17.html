Feb 17th-19th 2004 - Palm Beach, FL

At the end of every winter, like clockwork, my grandparents would pack the car, shake the New York snow off their coats and head south to Florida. This was back in the day of shuffleboard courts, orange groves and--poor Nana--unforgiving cotton swimsuits with pinching metal zippers. Explorers of distant lands, they said Florida was a different animal than Cape Cod, where my family spent a couple of weeks every summer. The beaches were longer, the water warmer and the shellfish sweeter. They had spoken so often of the Sunshine State's soft, white sand that nothing could have made us happier than the spring they returned with sacks of the stuff in their trunk, shoveled into neatly tied cotton sacks. My grandfather ceremoniously unleashed the bounty, pouring it into our sandbox. As promised, it was silken, cool and white. The stuff we'd previously shoveled through our Sandy Andy was a closer cousin to kitty litter than to this stuff.

Crossing the border, part of me still expected some glimpse of the Florida of the '50s. There were a few, but not many. I was excited to spot an orange grove, but it was the only one I saw there. There were a few quaint, if practical, touches, like the device at the rest stop that automatically shot water at your windshield to hose off the tacky coating of smushed bugs. Each squirt felt like a little howdy:  "Welcome to Jeb country." "Welcome to the land of stolen elections." "Welcome to the swamp."

It turned out we were visiting Florida at the same time as our pals Rick and Sue Ayre and their kids. We'd had plans to stop in to see Rick's parents, and his sister and her family, but luck would have it that we were there just at the right time for full-metal Ayeration. Martha, Andy and their kids live in Gulf Stream, right next to Palm Beach, where we holed up. It had been so long since we'd seen any Ayres that there was a ton of catching up to do. Rick's mom, Helen, who has been battling more than her share of health problems, looked very well and was lively company. Russ is as healthy and energetic as ever and continues in his reign as benevolent king grandpa. Martha has turned into a serious runner and though she always looked amazing now she now looks even more so. Andy was charming, even after long days doctoring, and was kind enough to geek out with me over our many architectural drawings. And Remy and Cosmo were patient as Rufus scurried around their house, stealing their toys. As truly great hosts do, Martha and Andy offered us the things we missed most on the road: wonderful home-cooked meal and use of their washing machine. (Hotels charge so much for laundry that you might as well throw out your dirty socks and underwear and buy new.) We left their house feeling very well cared for, indeed.

Rick and Sue were staying at the Ritz down the road, as we were. Their kids were at Martha's, doing the cousin thing. And so, we had the luxury of some late-night chats in the hotel's lounge. Both of them looked wonderful and seemed great and are much wrapped up in a business Rick is working on. It had been so long since we'd seen each other that it felt like we were communicating in headlines: Russell is getting married. Kate graduated. Colin has a bad ankle sprain...The stories filled in and the balance resumed. It felt perfectly normal to be sitting in a fancy hotel so far from home, fleshing out our continuing stories with broad brush strokes. Over the next couple of days, as they always are, they were completely generous in sharing their family with us, making us feel one of their bubbling brood.

But it would be an incomplete story if I failed to report on the hotel.

Obviously, we stay in lots and lots of hotels. I adore a good hotel. Fresh, fluffy towels! Fresh, fluffy robes! No question--I'm a huge fan of seamless service. Good hotels charge lots of money on the promise of delivering exactly that. So, when things go wrong, it really, really pisses me off. As I type those words, I'm recoiling at my own privileged, cranky babyheadedness. Bad service is a long way from being truly horrible thing, like, oh, say, subverting the democratic process or cancer or genocide. But right now, selfishly, our tiny road trip reality is defined in good part by life in hotels. And so...

We got to the Palm Beach Ritz around 8 p.m. We checked into our room, having driven a long day and feeling way too tired to explore the nearby town. We got Rufus settled in and headed for the hotel's restaurant. It was full up, indoors and out. We asked the hostess how long a wait it would be for a table.
"About twenty minutes," she replied, and suggested we get a drink at the bar while we waited.

No problem. We settled in with a couple a margaritas, the rumbling road slipping away with each sip. At about half an hour another hostess came over to us as we sat at the bar.

"Have you been helped?" she asked.

"Well, yes," we said, and then thought twice about the question. "I mean, we asked for a table about a half an hour ago."

"Oh!" she said, sounding genuinely put out. "I don't have any tables. You'll have to wait."

She bustled off in a huff.

Puzzling. She came up to us. Had she approached simply to taunt us about our tableless status?

We switched to sipping fizzy water, as we hadn't managed lunch that day and one drink was getting us hammered. At 45 minutes we asked hostess number one if a table was in the offing any time soon. She said she'd look into that. At 50 minutes, hostess number one offered us a table inside. Though we'd requested an outdoor table, we leapt at it. Then, hostess number two walked us to a table outside, apologizing all the while that it was all they had.

Clearly, they were trying to keep us of balance.

Our waiter took our order and offered more drinks. What with food on the horizon, perhaps another would go down nicely. The second round of margaritas appeared promptly, though it was the last thing that did. We were falsely encouraged that food would soon arrive when a server brought knives to the table. They ridiculously large, like something from the evidence table at the O.J. Simpson trial. And they were a sham--it would be eons before we'd need them.

Half an hour later our glasses were empty and we were so hungry we were ready to start asking folks at nearby tables for a bite or two. We questioned the hotel's wisdom in arming us.

I caught the waiter's eye and he came by the table.

"Would you have a bread basket?" I asked. "We've been here quite a while and we're really hungry."

"Oh, the bread looked a little stale, so I decided not to serve it," he replied and then scurried away.

Moments later, the table next to ours was magically delivered manna from heaven in the form of a bread basket. We watched as they sampled. Perhaps they had a more careless waiter, one less concerned about quality. But no. No teeth broke on its stale contents. In fact, no one looked even a bit unhappy about what they were eating. More time passed. I was ready to eat my hand. Once again, I caught our waiter's eye. He came back.

"It seems that they've found some bread worthy of serving in the kitchen," I said, pointing to the next table.

He noted the lucky diners at the next table, buttering rolls.

"I'll look into that," he said, inspiring little confidence as he once again disappeared.

Maybe 10 more minutes passed. A waiter we hadn't yet met delivered a plate of napkin-wrapped bread to our table.
Seconds later, our original waiter appeared and examined it.

"Oh," he said. "He brought you...different bread." Once again, he turned on his heel and vanished.

Different bread?

Moments later, actual food arrived. We had now been in the restaurant for two hours and--miracle of miracles--food was about to reach our lips. The dinner was fine. Serviceable, if unmemorable. Just as we finished, the evening picked up considerably: Rick and Sue appeared, as if on cue, from the gardens below the balcony where we were sitting.

Later on, happily Ayreated and returning to our room, we considered the next day's needs. We both require coffee immediately upon waking. To insure that, we thought we'd go ahead and use that little hang tag they leave in on your bed at night--the one for ordering the next day's breakfast to be delivered at a particular time. No such luck. Our room had no such tag. We had seen them hanging on other doors, so we knew they existed. And we considered calling to the front desk to get someone to bring one around, but we were sleepy. In an act of stupidity, we took our chances.

The following morning, relying on the bright, bright sun to give us our initial jumpstarting jolt, we took Rufus for a romp by the ocean. He was joyous--zipping along the breaking surf like a puppy. We didn't notice the "no dogs" sign until we were exiting the beach. Though it did explain the dour looks we got from a number of beach walkers. Undaunted, we strolled across the street with the panting Rufus in search of a coffee joint with a few outdoor tables and a bowl of water for fur boy. We did find such a place--a little mom and pop shop that had apparently had an unexpected morning rush: every table was filled with dirty dishes and no one was making a move to clear them. Still, they were nice enough. They brought water for Ru and coffee and scrambled eggs for us. The water glasses were thoughtfully marked with the fuschia lipstick favored by those who sipped from them before us, a fact I unfortunately discovered after draining half the glass.

Ick.

At a nearby table, a large 70-something fellow with a heavy NY accent bellowed into his cell phone, "I'm in FLORIDA. It's 70 degrees! It's Bee-you-tee-ful!" It was a cell phone conversation we would hear repeated numerous times during our stay in the sunshine state, each made by different callers in different places, but all with pretty much the same accent.

Our second day at The Ritz, still menu hang-tagless, we decided to try calling room service for breakfast. At least they were honest: it would take at least an hour for coffee to reach us, they apologized. Hoping to get on with the day a little sooner than that, we foolishly decided to try the hotel's restaurant again. As we walked up to the restaurant we saw the hallway was choked with people waiting to be seated. There was the young hostess from our first night there. The poor thing was looking just miserable.

"How long for a table?" I asked.

"About 20 minutes," she muttered, unable to make eye contact while lying. Then she added, almost whispering "maybe longer."

Another hostess--an uber hostess--appeared and started bossing the poor junior hostess around. As she did, I glanced down and counted 14 names ahead of ours on her list. Unless they seated someone every minute, this wasn't going to be a 20 minute wait. I stopped counting just in time to hear uber hostess tell her,

"If this party or this party asks for a table, seat them before anyone else."

She said this is clear earshot of at least 6 couples standing nearby. Amazed, and pondering the potential diners' possible mob connections, I was interrupted by the junior hostess.

"Do you want the buffet or do you want to order off the menu?" she asked.

"I don't know," I said. "We haven't eaten here before."

She handed us menus, which we were considering when Uber approached us.

It's important to note that this day--though clear--was very windy. No one was sitting out on the restaurant's balcony.

"We'd sit outside if you'll serve out there," I offered.

"OK,"

"So, which will it be? Buffet or from the menu," uber asked, taking the menus away from us. Mere diners can't be trusted with such powerful items.

"Um, we hadn't quite decided," I said.

"You'll sit outside, then," she said pointing us toward the doors.

"Could we have the menus back?" I asked.

Looking annoyed, she acquiesced.

It wasn't nearly as windy out on the balcony as expected. We found a protected corner and felt smug that we'd outwitted another horrific wait. It was lucky we decided on the buffet because nothing resembling service was coming our way. We got up and helped ourselves to food. One cup of coffee was poured by a waiter who disappeared, never to be seen again. I took to walking over to the balcony's coffee station--the one set up for the serving staff--to refill my cup. If anyone noticed, they didn't try to stop me. Just as well. I know where they keep the big knives.

Almost all we did that day was eat. Martha, Helen, Sue, Sarah and I had a fine ladies-who-lunch meal at The Sundy House, sitting out in an extraordinary lush, green garden. If we hadn't all been married--or too young to consider such a thing--we would have been driven to propose to each other. Extraordinary setting, wonderful food, great company. And Sarah held her own very well as a verging-on-grown-up member of the party. If I were to visit this part of the world again, I'd definitely stay here. It's splendid--cool structures, bright Caribbean colors and a garden that sports a swimming pool you share with tropical fish. (And yes--they take dogs.)

Eric and I followed lunch with a driving tour of the fancy mansions out in Palm Beach. Well, more accurately, we toured the gigantic walls of the fancy mansions. You might occasionally get a glimpse of garden, but rarely actual house. And who knew our over-the-top Lexus was the Ford Taurus of this part of the world? Those with more than a couple of million to rub together drive Rolls or Bentleys or something else equally obscenely expensive. We then wedged in a little gym time and rested up so we'd be fresh for the exceptional dinner Rick took us all out to at Cafe L'Europe in Palm Beach. We ordered well and plates circled the table for all to sample. My favorite was the quartet of soups I ordered, served in tiny tureens. I'm  immediately convinced that tiny tureens are a perfect vessel. (And the soups were beet, curry, minted fresh pea and cucumber dill--but saying that doesn't begin to do them justice.)

There was a private party going on in the room next to ours. As it drew to a close, a parade of waiters dimmed the lights and carried glowing, candlelit items into the celebration--most intriguing. I asked our waiter what they were. "Ice swans," he smiled, saying he'd try to find one for us after we'd finished our dinners. We'd gone to dinner late and we closed the joint. As promised, after we finished, two candlelit swans were presented to us, filled with chocolate-dipped strawberries, tiny chocolates and cookies. Shiny and happy!

Driving out of Palm Beach the next day, we contemplated life in such a place. The beaches were spectacular. The tropical climate guarantees green and lush. Lovely homes--older ones, like Martha and Andy's--have a lot of soul. But the rush to McMansionize the place is disturbing. Still, it looks like a good life. Just don't stay at The Ritz.

STATS

Number of ice swans served to us: 2
The hotel we'd never stay at again in Palm Beach: The Ritz Palm Beach
The hotel we'd choose instead: The Sundy House
