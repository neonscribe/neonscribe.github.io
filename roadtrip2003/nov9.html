Nov. 9th, 2003 -- Denver> through Kansas > Kansas City, Missouri

Sunday morning, we awoke in our room at The Brown Palace (I just know my sister is in stitches over that name) having fallen asleep to the local news. We'd failed to hit the sleep timer on the TV, so when we woke up it was still the local news. Oddly enough, it was news of our hotel. In a PR coup that's easy in even large media markets on a Sunday morning, a very French representative of the hotel was giving a demonstration of an event scheduled later that morning, down in our very own lobby. He was hawking the annual Brown Palace holiday season kickoff: the pouring of a massive champagne tower. Six floors below, an impressive pyramid of glasses stood at the ready. In the TV studio, a tiny, three-tier tower had been constructed right on the anchor's desk.

"It's really an art, cutting the top of a champagne bottle off with a saber," the hotel fellow explained. Who would argue such a point with a saber-wielding sommelier? But in the studio, a cork had already been safely manually popped. As he poured and the champagne cascaded, the anchor quickly grew nervous.

"It's going to spill everywhere!" she cried.

Isn't that just the way with too much fun? In no time at all, someone's upset.

We were facing an eleven-hour day in the car. And judging by the news anchor's response, we could only imagine the hysteria that might erupt among the local populace once they started beheading bottles back at the Brown Palace. We beat a hasty retreat.

The sky was gray but the road was dry. We found an NPR station and caught the end of Weekend Edition Sunday. A story on tasting durian, the stinkiest fruit known to man, was pretty delightfully silly. We were intrigued by an interview with David Macaulay about his latest book, Mosque. As luck would have it, this was followed by a story about former CBS correspondent Bill Kurtis's efforts to turn Sedan,Kansas into an artsy place that would draw tourists. It's located in the southeast part of the state, and the description of the plains version of Stonehenge made us want to go. We'll see what the next couple of days bring.

This wasn't a day for a whole lot of exploring; it was a day for covering a whole lot of territory. It was also the day I learned I could write in the car. Yesterday's entry was tapped out on my lap, with the landscape, such as it was, whizzing by outside the window. The iPod dutifully entertained with its shuffle-play delivery of folk rock and jazz.

We did pull off the highway to check out Goodland, whose claim to fame is its giant roadside easel holding a reproduction of Van Gogh's Sunflowers. We saw almost no signs as life as we drove the town's streets. Zip. No people. No moving cars. No dogs in yards. Nothing. It was a little eerie. We left before being sucked into whatever Twilight Zone madness was going on there.

Though we braved monkey pox by visiting Celestial Seasonings' free-range prairie dogs, and signs hawking Prairie Dog Town were just about the only thing breaking up the landscape for the better part of 100 miles, it was surprisingly easy to pass up its six-legged cow and 8,000 pound prairie dog come-on. We whizzed along, similarly undeterred by signs beckoning us to visit Bob Dole's hometown and the birthplace of Arlen Specter. Those billboards failed to mention how many legs they had, or how much they weighed.

With the highway so hypnotically straight, changing drivers every couple of hours becomes even more important than when the driving is more challenging. As we swapped seats we delighted in some of Kansas' lovelier truck stops.  Some offered just the kind of welcome you hope will be extended everywhere to armed guys wearing camoflage duds. We'll note these unlikely plastic trees were staked down, so the constant wind wouldn't carry them off a la Dorothy and Toto.

We finished listening to Al Franken. Somewhere around hour eight in the car we started thinking up prank calls we could make to the Lexus Link agent. We'd push the button and hear:

"How may I help you, Mr. and Mrs. Benson?"

"Do you have Prince Albert in a can?" we'd ask.

"Let me put you on hold for just a minute while I check," the excessively polite voice would respond.

That we giggled madly at the thought of this proves the power of the open road to break down your normal defenses and leave you stupid. Despite our diminished faculties, we crossed into Missouri and found our hotel. A strip steak and a half-bottle of wine later and we slept the sleep of the well-traveled, snug in our rented little bed.
