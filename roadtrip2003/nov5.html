Nov. 5th, 2003 -- Salt Lake City > Castle Valley

From Eric:
Breakfast at  Lamb's Grill with Jon, Alyssa, Ainsley, Aric and Sam. Checked out of the Monaco, filled up the car, hit the road at noon. Snow flurries but no accumulation, changing to actual sunshine in Price. Stopped at the UPS Store in Price. I was walking Rufus when a woman asked if he was a "Cor-jee". I said "Cor-gee". She said "Isn't there a children's book illustrator who...?" I said "Tasha Tudor". She said "You aren't from around here." (This was a statement, not a question.)

Made it to Castle Valley as the sun was setting. Dinner with Chrissie.

From Susan:
Lamb's Grill is the kind of treasure you hope every decent sized city will have: an old, downtown restaurant that hasn't much changed since its doors swung open in 1919. Pols and power brokers breakfast there. Walking in, you stroll by what feels like one of the longest lunch counters west of the Mississippi. It's easy to imagine decades of men in suits and hats chowing down on a slice of pie and a cuppa...well, this is Salt Lake City. Most of those guys would have had a glass of milk or a bracing cup of Postum. Today, there are as many sinners as saints, so the evil bean is swilled with abandon. Above the wall facing the counter, there's an equally long mirror, framed with carved, painted wood--a little bit craftsman, a little bit deco, and seemingly untouched since the teens.

Jon, Alyssa, Ainsley, Aric and Sam came in out of the snow to join us there for breakfast this morning. We commandeered the restaurant's back room, which was in that happy lull between the breakfast and lunch rushes. This allowed kids to be kids and the baby to be a baby without other diners wishing us a speedy meal and rapid departure. Though none of the staff took a dive while we were there, they were able to satisfy Aric's craving for a hot dog, something that The Mayan--with all its glitz and singing birds--had failed to do. Aric was dressed in his Batman PJs, quite a concession from a guy who prefers doing most things au naturel.

At one point during the meal, Aric looked up and asked "Where's my cape?"

"In my pack," Alyssa answered. "Do you need it?"

"No," he said, and returned to peeling his hot dog.

I loved this exchange. That Alyssa took the request entirely seriously and understood the notion of needing the cape was touching. And I loved that there was a moment during dining when a young superhero-in-training just needed to know that his cape was accounted for. Eggs and waffles were eaten, hot chocolate served in tiny, scorchingly hot carafes was sucked through straws. It was time to go.

We all trundled back to our hotel so the kids could say goodbye to Rufus and climb around in the insanely large bathtub in our room. I think of what I remember of being that young. I'm sure I'd remember a bathtub as massive as this one for years to come. Alyssa and I considered what a nice sandbox it would make; if you turned on the jets, you could have your very own sandstorm.

We made our goodbyes--a little tough, as it just didn't feel like enough time together. Ainsley promised to e-mail me a picture of the snowman she built in her back yard. And once again I undertook the trunk packing ritual, this time, to the compliments of the hotel's doorman, who suggested I might have a future in this business. He then added that it was a future I probably wouldn't want to have. It's the kind of painfully honest comment that's meant to be funny but just makes you sad. It made me think of the cab we'd seen the previous evening. On its bumper, was a sticker soliciting drivers reading, "Drive a Taxi: Live the Dream."

I lived in Salt Lake City from 1973 to 1982, harboring a dream of leaving for most of those years. That I stayed is testament to Eric's vast charm. I used to sit on the circle on the University of Utah campus watching the car tail lights driving west on I-80 toward Nevada, envying the drivers their escape. My 19 year-old self knew they were headed to things far more exciting than could ever happen in this odd, conservative town I now called home. It's hard to explain the cultural strangeness of Salt Lake to someone who hasn't lived there. And there's an instant bond when you find someone who has: they get stuff others won't. It's way beyond, "you're from New York? I'm from New York, too!" To say the L.D.S. Church is a dominant force doesn't quite touch it, even though that's certainly at the heart of it. To live on the edges of a force field that's so restrictive and pervasive, but not really be a part of it is inherently alienating, even if joining up is something you'd loathe. A lot has changed in Salt Lake. You don't have to be Mormon to be elected in Salt Lake anymore, but it probably still helps. In yesterday's mayoral race, the incumbent, Rocky Anderson won. He's not LDS. And he managed to win even though he  really pissed off the LDS voting block during his last term; a whopping 85 percent of them voted for his opponent. Anyway, every time I leave Salt Lake, I do so with a strong sense of having spent so much time wanting to go, and now, being able to. And here's the kicker: now, I really quite like the place. Go figure.

The drive from Salt Lake to Chrissie's house in Castle Valley was easy. The snow, though falling pretty steadily, wasn't sticking. Once it cleared, we were quickly in the land of vast plains and towering rock. The earth and the rock is a stunningly pretty rusty clay color. And the road to Castle Valley, which winds along the Colorado River, is about as lovely a drive as exists anywhere. Every turn reveals a more breathtaking series of rock towers and cliffs. Bumping along the road to Chrissie's we always have the same conversation. It contains the words "gorgeous," "breathtaking" and "wow."
