Nov. 14th & 15th -- St. Louis

Our pal Rick went to college in Missouri and frequently visits his older kids in St. Louis. When we asked him what we should do in the city with the big, big arch, he said that outside of visiting family, there wasn't much reason to go there. If you're driving the route we are, there isn't much choice. We put up in a hotel in Clayton, an affluent suburb with the St. Louis skyline glittering in the distance. From our hotel window, The Gateway Arch defined an otherwise unexceptional block of skyscrapers. The arch has the same sort of improbable charm as the Space Needle, so we were kindly disposed. After considering the options the town offered-- touring the Budweiser plant, visiting the Museum of Westward Expansion, riding a paddle wheeler, dropping in on a casino, or visiting the aforementioned arch--we had a pang for Rufus, who couldn't do any of those things. We settled on something unique to St. Louis that we could all do: taking in the art at American Kennel Club Museum of the Dog.

There was just one little catch--we had meant to give Ru a bath before leaving Seattle. Matters only got worse in Montana, where he rolled in something with a mighty stink. (Dead squirrel or bear shit are the leading contenders.) It was too damn cold for a full immersion. Spot cleaning was time consuming and disgusting, but reasonably effective. 15 or 20 baby wipes later and we were able to happily share a car again. Still, Ru looked grubby, at best. And we were about to take him to a place run by snooty folk--the D.A.R. of the canine world; a place where appearances mattered more than almost anything.

Rufus has the breeding to cut it with the A.K.C.: his parents are prize-winning, muckety-muck Corgis.(Pa-pa was Cypress Evening Shadow; ma-ma had three names, too.) But in the eyes of the organization, he's a bastard: we never bothered to register him and never thought much about it. But now, when he was about to weather a critical, trained eye, I was consumed with the need to bathe him and knock his toenails back to a decent length. It appeared the fates were smiling on us: there was a groomer just a block from our hotel.

Now, Rufus has been to a groomer exactly twice in his nine years of dogdom. Once, before my sister and Ken were married and all went well. (He was best dog at their wedding and never looked lovelier.) The second time, I think the groomer did something horrible while cutting his toenails and Ru has been neurotic as hell about pedicures ever since. From that point on, we stopped using "pros." Washing the dog in the hotel tub would result in an aftermath of grief for the hotel maid, so we decided to take him to a groomer and stay with him while he was being prettied up, making sure nothing untoward happened on the road to foofyness.

We entered the "Dog Spa," a tiny cinder block bunker of a space. A large, cheerful young woman in a tie-dyed t-shirt greeted us. At least we assumed she greeted us. She was smiling, anyway. In fact, we couldn't hear a word. A huge, crated yellow lab unleashed a wall of ear-splitting, high-pitched barking the moment we walked in.

"We called about getting the dog groomed?" I said to her.

She looked at me quizzically.

"I CALLED A FEW MINUTES AGO," I shouted.

She nodded. "YOU'RE THE PEOPLE WHO WANTED TO STAY WITH THE DOG WHILE IT GETS WASHED?" she shouted back.

I nodded.

We all considered the hysterical lab. Every fiber of its being was devoted to ensuring our collective hearing loss. The tie-dyed groomer handed me a questionnaire and a pen.

What was Rufus' temperament?

"Generally good," I wrote, thinking that our postman, Robert William, or the pizza delivery guy might have a different answer. Rufus wants a piece of both of them and we all know it.

Our little victim was now tugging at his leash, trying to get back out the door we'd just come in. He knew a bum deal when he saw it. That lab had spilled the beans--this was a torture chamber. We heard "Yip-yip-yip-yip-yip!" But Rufus heard, "Run away! Run away NOW!"

In addition to our names and addresses, the questionnaire asked if Ru had any allergies. "Wheat," I penciled in.

Who was his vet?

Dr. Canfield of Union Bay Animal Hospital. I didn't have his address with me.

The yelping continued. I turned to Eric and said, "This is too much. Should we go?"

He shook his head, "no."

Tie-dye woman made a show of reading the questionnaire. The yelping was bouncing off every hard surface in the joint, which was pretty much every surface.

"SO, HE DOESN'T HAVE ANY ALLERGIES?" she yelled.

I pointed to the "wheat" entry on the questionnaire.

She nodded. Then she tried to make friends with Ru by slipping him a dog cookie. I shook my head in the negative, tapping my pen on the "wheat" reply.

I pointed to the vet and the empty address and phone, shrugging my shoulders and raising my hands in a bad mime's "I-don't-have-a-clue" gesture.

She nodded again, waving off the ommission and taking the paper from me. Yellow lab's yip fit was in full crescendo. I considered yanking the questionnaire back. A desire to tear it up and shove wads of paper into my ears was slowly but surely building.

Sensing our discomfort, she yelled, "IF I HAD EARPLUGS, I'D GIVE YOU SOME."

We smiled sympathetically. If anything, yellow lab was getting up a head of steam--there was no sign of the barking letting up.

"IS THERE A SEPARATE ROOM WHERE THE DOGS ARE WASHED? SOMEPLACE...QUIETER?" I queried.

"NOT REALLY," she answered.

"I can't take this," Eric suddenly said. "Let's get out of here."

"I'M SORRY--THIS ISN'T GOING TO WORK," Eric shouted at her.

We opened the door and Rufus was out it as soon as it swung wide enough for him to squeeze through. Back in the car, Lexus Link was most helpful in finding a happily downscale alternative.

"You want The U-Do-It Dogwash, Mr.and Mrs. Benson," the disembodied voice chimed helpfully from the car's ceiling. "Is there anything else I can do for you today?"

It hardly seemed fair to ask him to wash the dog. So we drove the eight miles across town in search of next-to-Godliness for Ru. No bother. Eight miles of blissful silence enveloped us as we bumped over railroad tracks and drove through neighborhoods of small, tidy houses.

We were fast becoming connoisseurs of dog grooming architecture. U-Do-It had cunningly aped the bunker look so popular in the tonier joint across town. Inside, for a bargain $8, we were handed black rubber aprons that would have served well for our ER cameos as autopsy room pathologists. Two threadbare towels, thoughtfully pre-coated in black fur were part of the package, as was a luxury cannister vacuum cleaner, set on reverse.

"Do you want to to blow some of that stray hair off first?" the business's owner asked.

Rufus isn't all that much for vacuums when they're taking on rugs, so we said we'd just forge ahead, popping Ru into the counter height sink, with sprayer. The whole thing was far easier than our usual strip-down-and-get-in-the-shower routine from home. There was no indication that the wriggling Rufus found it any easier. Clearly, he was not at all pleased with the turn the day had taken. We scrubbed. We rinsed. We fluffed. We manicured. In short order, Rufus was looking as handsome as he gets. And so, off we drove for a heady dose of dog art.

The Museum of the Dog sits on the outskirts of St. Louis, in horse country. (The Budweiser Clydesdales live just down the way.) It's housed in a turn-of-the-century home donated to the organization by a departed dog lover. Clearly, they're well endowed: there's a significant addition and a pretty large collection of all manner of dog paraphernalia.

Rufus strutted up to the front door, excited to enter. He received the requisite fussing from the woman taking tickets. When I asked her about her pup, she replied, "Oh, I have a cat."

Go figure.

Rufus expressed huge curiosity about a meeting room with a closed door. Cat woman was nice enough to open the doors for him, so he could go exploring.

Ever astute, Eric asked, "Was there food in here recently?"

"Why, yes," she replied.

Rufus made sure no crumbs remained before we worked the gallery rooms.

The collection was pretty random. Oil paintings ranged from the traditional to the modern to the humorous. Similarly, sculpture caught the many moods of the dog, from relaxed to playful. Corgis were well represented, both framed and free standing. A show on human aid dogs was quite touching, as was an exhibit in the basement gallery on the dogs of war.

Sated and exhausted by the day's efforts, we returned to our digs for a good night's sleep. Rolling out of town the next morning, we made the tourism equivalent of a drive-by shooting, with our camera playing the part of the gun. The arch was very big. Beer was nowhere to be seen. And a paddle wheeler was just pulling out into the muddy river. The buildings on the edges of town are about as perfectly decaying as any we've seen in the U.S. As St. Louis gave way to nondescript burbs and finally green rolling fields, we were happy to think about Louisville waiting at the end of the day's drive.
