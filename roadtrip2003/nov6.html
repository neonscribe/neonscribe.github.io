Nov. 6th, 2003 -- Castle Valley, Utah


Lying in bed looking out Chrissie's bedroom window is like having the previous night's dream continue into waking. It looks like another planet out there. This house is tucked under a huge cliff and surrounded by towering red rock formations that jut into the sky for miles. (It takes pluck to build under a cliff: here's what you hope doesn't happen.) Up here above the valley, scrubby desert vegetation thrives and  the occasional house peeks up in the distance. Bunnies lop by. So do deer and coyotes. And there's a huge bull snake in residence that grows fat dining on baby squirrel. If you're thinking, "poor baby squirrels" you flunk the residency test: anything that keeps the rodent population down is a good thing. Outside, the occasional car driving through on a road a mile away sounds incredibly loud. Doors are left unlocked and car keys are left in the ignition. It's hard to believe there are still places where this is true. It's equally hard to believe that anyone is allowed to build here; it's like living in the middle of a national park.

Water is a big issue. When Chrissie built here more than a decade ago, she did so thinking she could drill down, find some water and put in a well. That's what most folks do. Unfortunately, many attempts to tap into water came up, quite literally, dry. And so, a guy in Moab, some 17 miles away, trucks in her water. One 4,000 gallon tank lasts several months in fall and winter. In summer, it's about a month's worth. (Most goes to irrigating a tiny bit of planting around the house.) And Chrissie is conservative in ways even those of us who lived through California droughts find impressive. The toilet is flushed when absolutely necessary. Dipping in the hot tub helps keep you clean; a shower is something for every few days. You never leave a tap running when you're washing your hands. She does have a small, high-efficiency washer, and a tiny dishwasher drawer that uses very little water. When she offered us use of her washing machine, we felt the love. That's a precious commodity. We protested, but she insisted. Then we had the pleasure of hanging stuff out on the line to dry. This is something most of us have stopped doing, and stupidly so. It saves energy and your clothes smell great. (Granted, it's not for Seattle in winter. But in summer, there's no excuse not to.)

Today brought sun--dim, but unmistakably there. We fueled up on coffee and headed out for a leisurely hike. We debated going to Fisher Towers, a favorite place, but decided on checking out Castleton Tower to take a peek at some property a couple of miles away that Chrissie--and many others--bought up and donated to a Nature Conservancy-like trust, The Castle Rock Collaboration. We passed climbers sorting through ropes and gear and the occasional camper, all of whom were just hanging out, staring at the immense rocks. There's fine, clay colored sand everywhere. When you walk, it works its way into your shoes, through your socks and between your toes. Back at the ranch, when I take off my shoes, the bottoms of my feet look like I've dropped into a henna tattoo parlor and asked them to apply the stuff to my soles with a paint roller. Rufus' white paws and belly have taken on a distinctively pink tinge, too.

After the hike Eric and I drive down the River Road into Moab a small, sweet, albeit crunchy town that is mountain bike central station spring through fall. Now though, things have quieted down. The parking lots of the many motels lining the town's main street are mostly empty. There's a decent organic market, a bunch of cafes, a couple of  bookstores--one with a great magazine stand. Though, clearly, they so often have to explain to out-of-towners why the NY Times is a day old that they've posted a flow chart of the steps involved in getting the paper from the printing plant in LA to this corner of eastern Utah. There are crafts shops with better-than-average pottery and paintings. The place looks far more prosperous than it did when we passed through here a few years back. But at its border, it still tips its hat to the guy who, for many people, put the area on the map: The Duke. An ancient sign for The Apache Motel invites you to "stay where John Wayne stayed." As their site says, "Why pay more buckaroos when you can enjoy the same amenities at the Apache Motel for less?"   Why, indeed? Hell, if you're feeling flush, book the John Wayne suite. John Ford stayed there first, but he didn't rate a suite.  Poor buckaroo. And after all he did to turn the place into Hollywood's real live backdrop...

Stats:

Hours driven: 1
Hours ogling rock: 4, give or take
Showers taken: 0
Movies watched: 1 (Bachelor Mother--swell screwball comedy starring Ginger Rogers)
